Brand Story

The Legacy of
John C. Dunham

Born from the industrial precision of Equipto — the Aurora-based world leader in steel shelving and space utilization — JCD Foundation carries the initials and the mission of John C. Dunham himself. Under his leadership, Equipto became a global manufacturing powerhouse, and Dunham applied that same systematic thinking to building a better community.

After studying Just-in-Time manufacturing principles in Japan, Dunham understood that the greatest waste is unrealized human potential. He believed skilled trades, health sciences, and manufacturing were not secondary paths — they were the backbone of a thriving community.

JCD Foundation is the Applied Legacy — the specialized, high-impact expression of that belief. While the broader Dunham Foundation addresses systemic regional capacity, JCD focuses precisely on the individual's path: from Fox Valley classroom to career-ready professional.

Minimum Size

Never reproduce the logo below 40px height in digital applications or 0.5 inches in print. Below this threshold, fine detail in the mark is lost.

Clear Space

Maintain a minimum clear space equal to the height of the "J" letterform on all four sides of the logo. No text, graphics, or other logos may intrude on this zone.

Approved Backgrounds

Use on White (#FFFFFF), Ice Gray (#F0F3F7), Equipto Blue (#0E2A58), and Deep Charcoal (#111827) only. Never place on a photographic background without an opaque overlay.

Prohibited Modifications

Do not rotate, stretch, recolor, add drop shadows, apply gradients, or alter the proportions of the logo in any way. Use only the approved files.

The Case for a New Mark

Why Change What We Have

Current mark · "JCD" wordmark + swoosh · c. 2023
Stress test · actual pixel sizes

Here's what happens when it shrinks.

The chrome gradient flattens to a gray blur. The swoosh ribbon turns into a smudge. The italic letters lose their silver-to-white contrast. By 24px, the mark is unreadable.

Verdict · Requires redesign for small-size use
Observation 01
The style dates us.

Chrome gradient + italic + swoosh-ribbon is the visual vocabulary of 1998–2008 — used widely for auto-detailing shops, small-league sports teams, and mid-market corporate. It reads "established small business," which is not the message for a foundation investing in the next generation of tradespeople.

Observation 02
The chrome can't survive production.

The silver-to-white gradient that gives the current mark its lift disappears the moment it hits single-color print, embroidery, laser etching, or small-scale screen. The "flat" SVG we trace from it loses the shine and leaves a heavy, cramped silhouette.

Observation 03
There's no icon-only version.

Every modern brand needs a reduced mark for favicons, app icons, social avatars, and small-scale signage. The current logo is a wordmark-only lockup at a wide aspect ratio (1782 × 934) — it simply cannot compress into a square without becoming illegible.

Observation 04
It doesn't signal craft.

JCD funds welders, nurses, carpenters, electricians — people who make things with their hands. The current mark signals "corporate swoosh," not "craft." There's no visual connection to tools, work, trades, or the people we serve.

Observation 05
It fights the brand voice.

Our defined tone is editorial-poster × streetwear × Portra documentary — honest, gritty, confident, a little rough around the edges. The chrome-and-swoosh is the opposite: polished, corporate, trying a little too hard. Every touchpoint we build feels at war with the logo sitting on top of it.

Observation 06
Equity is still low — so the cost is low.

JCD is a new foundation. Scholarship count is still small, external recognition of the mark is minimal, print runs are limited. The moment to reset is now, before the current mark lives on thousands of banners, transcripts, and grad photos and becomes harder to replace.

Opportunity · Not problem

The logo we have is a 2004 logo. We're building a 2026 foundation for the next generation of skilled-trades talent. The eight concepts in the Logo Lab below are an honest attempt to meet this moment — the work, the people, the craft — with a mark that earns its place on a welder's apron, a nurse's name badge, and a 16-pixel browser tab.

Logo Lab · Round 2

Eight Directions, Two Engines

This is exploration, not decision. The current stencil monogram + quadrant seal pair in §Identity is the standing direction — these concepts are a round-two widening to pressure-test it before we vectorize anything.

We ran the same six directional briefs through two image engines: Nano Banana (Gemini 3 Pro Image) and OpenAI Image (gpt-image). Each has a different accent — NB tends documentary and in-context, OpenAI tends cleaner and more graphic. Both views are useful.

Read each card's tradeoff note before picking favorites. A logo that photographs well on a leather apron may not shrink to 24px on a nav bar. Our job is to find the one that does both.

Reading the results. The stencil (#01), chop-stamp (#05), and quadrant seal (#02) are the three directions that held together at true 16-pixel display — which means they're the only three that can carry a full multi-channel identity today without a separate icon system. Tool-forged (#03), dataplate (#04), and heritage shield (#06) are viable with a paired simplified favicon variant, which is a normal and acceptable path for many strong brands.

Brand Voice
